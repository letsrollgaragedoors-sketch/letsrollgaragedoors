7 Warning Signs Your Garage Door Spring Is About to Break

June 2, 2026 Let's Roll Team 5 min read

A broken torsion spring is one of the most common, and most dangerous, garage door failures. When a spring snaps, your door can become completely inoperable in seconds, leaving your vehicle trapped or your home unsecured. The good news: springs usually give you warning signs before they fail entirely.

Never attempt to repair or replace garage door springs yourself. Torsion springs are under extreme tension and can cause serious injury. Always call a certified technician.

A torsion spring system showing the gap from a broken spring. Never attempt repairs yourself, always call a certified technician.

1. The Door Feels Unusually Heavy

Springs counterbalance the weight of your garage door. If a spring is weakening or partially broken, the door will feel much heavier than normal when you try to open it manually. A balanced door should lift easily with one hand, if it takes real effort, that's a red flag.

2. You Hear a Loud Bang

When a torsion spring snaps, it makes a sound like a gunshot or loud bang, often described as someone throwing something heavy in the garage. If you hear this sound and your door won't open, your spring has likely broken.

3. There's a Visible Gap in the Spring

Visible gaps in a torsion spring coil indicate a broken spring that needs immediate replacement.

Look at the springs above your garage door. A broken torsion spring will have a visible gap, typically a separation of 2 inches or more in the coil. If you see this, don't use the door until it's repaired.

4. The Door Opens Crooked

If one spring fails in a two-spring system, the door will rise unevenly, one side will be higher than the other. This puts enormous strain on the cables, tracks, and opener, and can cause secondary damage quickly.

5. The Opener Strains or Fails

Your garage door opener is designed to work with a properly functioning spring system. If the opener is straining, reversing immediately, or triggering its safety stop when trying to open the door, a failing spring is often the cause.

6. Cables Are Loose or Hanging

Broken springs cause the cables to go slack or hang freely on the sides of the door. Loose cables are both a symptom and a danger, they can snap or tangle, causing additional damage.

7. The Spring Looks Stretched or Worn

Over time, springs lose tension and may appear stretched, elongated, or corroded. If the coils look widely spaced or the spring has visible rust, it's nearing the end of its lifespan and should be replaced proactively.

How Long Do Garage Door Springs Last?

Standard torsion springs are rated for approximately 10,000 cycles. For an average household opening the door 4 times a day, that's roughly 7 years. High-cycle springs rated for 25,000-50,000 cycles are available and worth the investment.

5 Easy Garage Door Maintenance Tips That Add Years to Its Life

May 15, 2026 Let's Roll Team 3 min read

Your garage door is one of the hardest-working mechanical systems in your home, often opening and closing 1,500+ times per year. A little preventative maintenance goes a long way. Here are five things you can do in under 30 minutes to extend its life and avoid expensive repairs.

1. Lubricate the Moving Parts Every 6 Months

Use a white lithium grease or silicone-based lubricant (not WD-40) on springs, hinges, rollers, and the opener chain or screw. Avoid lubricating the tracks, they should stay clean and dry for proper roller contact.

Apply lubricant to hinges, springs, and rollers, never to the tracks themselves.

2. Test the Balance Monthly

Disconnect the opener (pull the red cord) and manually lift the door halfway. It should stay in place. If it falls or shoots up, the springs are out of balance and need professional adjustment.

3. Inspect and Replace Weather Stripping

Check the rubber seal at the bottom of the door and the strips along the sides and top. Cracked or flattened seals let in water, pests, and hot Texas air. Replacement strips cost under $30 at any hardware store and install in minutes.

4. Tighten All the Hardware

The vibration of thousands of open/close cycles loosens nuts, bolts, and brackets over time. With a socket wrench, go around and snug up every fastener you can see, especially the bolts holding the track brackets to the wall.

5. Test Your Safety Sensors

Place a 2x4 flat on the floor in the path of the door and try to close it. The door should reverse immediately on contact. If it doesn't, the auto-reverse is malfunctioning and needs immediate attention, this is a safety requirement, not optional.

Pro tip: Schedule a professional tune-up once a year. Our techs catch issues you'd never notice, and it's far cheaper than an emergency repair call.

How Austin's Heat & Humidity Affect Your Garage Door

April 22, 2026 Let's Roll Team 3 min read

Central Texas summers are brutal, triple-digit heat, intense UV, and humidity that swings dramatically. Your garage door feels every bit of it. Here's what Austin homeowners need to know to protect their investment through every season.

Heat Warps Wood Panels

Wooden garage doors and those with wood overlays are especially vulnerable to Texas summers. Prolonged heat exposure causes wood to warp, crack, and separate, especially on south- or west-facing garages with direct afternoon sun exposure. UV-resistant sealant applied annually can significantly extend panel life.

Metal Expands and Contracts

Steel and aluminum doors expand in heat and contract at night. Over years, this thermal cycling loosens fasteners, warps tracks slightly, and causes seals to degrade faster than in milder climates. Quarterly hardware checks are more important here than in most parts of the country.

South and west-facing garage doors in Austin receive the most sun exposure and need extra protection.

Rubber Seals Degrade Fast

The bottom seal and weatherstripping on your door are made of rubber, and rubber doesn't love Texas heat. UV exposure hardens and cracks seals over time, letting in hot air, water, and insects. Plan to replace seals every 2-3 years in Austin vs. every 5 years in cooler climates.

Humidity and Springs

Austin's humidity, especially during spring storm season, accelerates corrosion on uncoated torsion springs. If your springs aren't galvanized or powder-coated, consider upgrading to corrosion-resistant springs at next replacement. They cost slightly more but last significantly longer in humid Texas air.

Seasonal Maintenance Schedule for Austin

  • Spring (March-May): Inspect seals, lubricate all hardware, check spring tension before summer heat sets in.
  • Summer (June-Aug): Check opener heat sensors, verify door balance, inspect door finish for sun damage.
  • Fall (Sept-Nov): Replace any degraded seals, tighten hardware, test auto-reverse.
  • Winter (Dec-Feb): Rare freezes can contract metal and crack brittle seals, inspect after any freeze event.
